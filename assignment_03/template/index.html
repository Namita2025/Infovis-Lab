VVS Line Delay Behavior Explorer

Key Findings

The majority of VVS S-Bahn stations act as delay amplifiers — trains depart later than they arrive. The S6 corridor stations show the strongest amplification: Malmsheim (+0.83 min), Neuwirtshaus (+0.64 min), and Weilimdorf (+0.58 min) consistently add the most delay per stop. High-traffic inner-city stations like Stadtmitte (5,340 trains/week, +0.11 min) and Bad Cannstatt (4,683 trains/week, +0.22 min) amplify moderately but affect a far greater volume of passengers.

A clear pattern of terminus and branching stations acting as absorbers emerges at the network's edges. Renningen recovers the most delay (−0.57 min), followed by Marbach (−0.43 min) and Rohr (−0.30 min) — all end-of-line or low-frequency stops where scheduled buffer time allows trains to recover before turning around. Waiblingen (3,004 trains/week, −0.18 min) is notably the largest absorber by traffic volume.

Filtering by line reveals that S6 drives the most amplification along the western Stuttgart corridor, while S4 and S60 show predominantly neutral or absorbing behavior at shared stations like Stadtmitte (S60: −0.22 min, S4: −0.11 min). This suggests that line frequency and platform sharing — not station size alone — are the primary drivers of delay accumulation in the VVS network.
